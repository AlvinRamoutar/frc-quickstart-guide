FIRST Robotics Competition

FIRST Robotics is at its core, a program given birth to by the outstanding team at FIRST, whose mission involves empowering youth For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology. Today, it has grown to much more. FRC and other FIRST programs are shaping communities, putting technology into the hands of unprivileged youth and challenging minds across the world to become more than just a 'user' of technology, but rather an innovator.

True to its namesake, FIRST promotes these values through competitions, the FIRST Robotics Competition (FRC). FRC is an event, a sport where robots are the players, working together with their alliance against opponents to achieve specific objectives identified in the annual challenge.

Ironically, the challenge involves teams building Robots. Luckily, FIRST has prepared specialized kits for teams which help accelerate their build process. Along this equipment is a lot of cool stuff provided on behalf of FIRST sponsors. A key set of this equipment provided would be the FRC Control System Hardware - both expensive and daunting at first.

NI RoboRIO

The NI-roboRIO is the main robot controller used for FRC. The roboRIO includes a dual-core ARM Cortexâ„¢-A9 processor and FPGA which runs both trusted elements for control and safety as well as team-generated code. Integrated controller I/O includes a variety of communication protocols (Ethernet, USB, CAN, SPI, I2C, and serial) as well as PWM, servo, digital I/O, and analog I/O channels used to connect to robot peripherals for sensing and control. The roboRIO should connect to the dedicated 12V port on the Power Distribution Panel for power. Wired communication is available via USB or Ethernet.

The grand, flat, and all-powerful brain of your robot; roboRIO. Meant to be the successor to the compact RIO (cRIO), the roboRIO is even more compact at a form factor of around 15cm x 15cm x 3cm. The best analogy for the roboRIO would be a tricked-out Arduino. Both are microcontroller units, with the roboRIO supporting much more functionality, such as on-board Ethernet and some useful safety features (e.g. Brownout behavior).


Features

  1. Digital Input and Output (DIO) connectors
    Handles digital input (HIGH or LOW signals). Used primary for interfacing sensors to your robotic system, ideal for switches (e.g. Limit Switches). Teams often make the mistake of using DIO to drive motors or other devices. This is NOT the intention of the DIO rail, and doing so WILL DAMAGE your roboRIO. DIO outputs a sinking/sourcing current of 4mA, meant solemnly for digital signals, and not for powering devices. If you need to power devices, either pull from the PDP, or the VRM.
  2. RS-232 Port
    Used for serial communication.
  3. I2C
    Used for serial communication. I've seen teams use this for Arduino/roboRIO interfacing.
  4. CAN Port
    Stands for Controller Area Network, allows for devices (often, microcontrollers) to communicate with each other without a host machine. This bus is used to connect your roboRIO to your PCM, and your PCM to your PDP, giving them the ability to communicate with each other.
  5. Power Connector
    Connects over to the PDP, VIN for the roboRIO. Rated at 7-16V at 45W max.
  6. USB Device Port
    Best way to upload code to and from your roboRIO.
  7. USB Host Retention Mounts
  8. USB host ports
  9. Ethernet Port
    Connects the roboRIO to a network. Assigned an address by your robot radio (router). If there was a problem doing so, the RIO will have an auto configured IP.
  10. Serial Peripheral Interface (SPI) Port
    Used for serial communication with peripherals, such as complex sensors (e.g. Gyro).
  11. Status LEDs
    Used for serial communication with peripherals, such as complex sensors (e.g. Gyro).
  12. Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) Connectors
    Used to communicate with speed controllers and other PWM driven systems.
  13. myRIO Expansion Port (MXP)
    Used to connect with breakout boards and custom circuits/systems. Essentially, if you want to create a custom 'attachment' or device that interfaces directly with the roboRIO. Teams often use this port for custom breadboard circuits, such as a custom LED light show. Be careful with how you use XMP; it is important that all systems are controlled BY the roboRIO at all times. Your XMP use will be inspected!
  14. XMP retention mount
  15. User and Reset buttons
    RESET restarts the roboRIO processor and FPGA. Similar to the reset button on an Arduino. USER button produces a logical FALSE while pressed, and TRUE otherwise. Often used for debugging as a manual, physical trigger.
  16. Analog Input
    Used for connecting devices that have an analog output, such as certain sensors (e.g. LDR).
  17. Relay Connectors
    Used for connecting relays. What's the difference between relays and speed controllers?
  18. Robot Signal Light (RSL) Port
    All FRC robots must come equipped with a provided standard LED lamp, mounted where easily visible. This light flashes depending on roboRIO status.

Tips

  • The roboRIO will have a plethora of wires situated around it, since most components of your robotic system will in one way or another interface with it. For this reason, it is recommended that the roboRIO be the central component to your control system board with ample space between itself, and other components.
  • The roboRIO module should be physically protected - unless if you want to dish out another $1000 for it.
  • The roboRIO should be easily accessible; especially during the competition. Make the lives of those in your pit easier!
  • The recommended mounting technique for the roboRIO is zip ties on the bottom, through the mounting spots on the roboRIO, and through your control system board.
  • For optimal operation, use a fully charged battery (especially before a match!). A low battery is asking for brownout when you need your bot the most. I highly recommend you check out the 'Brownout Behavior' section of the roboRIO User Manual.

Power Distribution Panel (PDP)

The Power Distribution Panel (PDP) is designed to distribute power from a 12VDC battery to various robot components through auto-resetting circuit breakers and a small number of special function fused connections. The PDP provides 8 output pairs rated for 40A continuous current and 8 pairs rated for 30A continuous current. The PDP provides dedicated 12V connectors for the roboRIO, as well as connectors for the Voltage Regulator Module and Pneumatics Control Module. It also includes a CAN interface for logging current, temperature, and battery voltage.

Most systems requiring power are to be connected to the PDP. The PDP is more than just a power distributor; it's also an intelligent circuit breaker.


Features

  1. PCM, VRM, and roboRIO Channels
    Channels rated for 20 Amps (PCM, VRM) and 10 Amps (roboRIO). Weidmuller connectors.
  2. Vbat Output Channels
    16 output channels (+/- pair) split 8/8, where channels 4-11 are rated for 30 amps, while channels 0-3, 12-15 are rated for 40 amps. For an output channel to be usable, it must be first outfitted with a fuse of the appropriate amperage. WAGO connectors.
  3. CAN
    Connects the PDP to the roboRIO/PDP/PCM controller area network. Weidmuller connectors.
  4. Input Power
    Connects to the 12V battery via battery connectors (Bushings Lugs).

Tips

  • When mounting, I recommend leaving ample space for the power rails to accommodate for heavy gauge wiring, as well as have the battery terminal mount facing outwards from your main control (mounting) board. This will ensure the 6 AWG wire for the battery will interfere the least with other wiring/component placements.
  • To fasten, the PDP has holes on each corner. A bolt and nut would suffice.
  • It is recommended that the PDP be the final node in your CAN chain, since it has a terminating resistor. As such, your wiring will be as follows: roboRIO > PCM > PDP

Pneumatics Control Module (PCM)

The PCM is a device that contains all of the inputs and outputs required to operate 12V or 24V pneumatic solenoids and the on board compressor. The PCM is enabled/disabled by the roboRIO over the CAN interface. The PCM contains an input for the pressure sensor and will control the compressor automatically when the robot is enabled and a solenoid has been created in the code. The device also collects diagnostic information such as solenoid states, pressure switch state, and compressor state. The module includes diagnostic LEDâ€™s for both CAN and the individual solenoid channels.

Depending on its use, the pneumatics system tends to be one of the most powerful, simple, and dangerous. On the surface, there isn't much to it; compressed air is pumped into a piston, forcing its expansion. However, unlike speed controllers which burn out in a stroke of heat and fit of smoke, invalid pneumatic systems explode. In FRC, ample resources have been provided for setting up the pneumatics system, along with regulatory equipment.
Unlike other modules in your robotic system, the PCM is optional, only needed if there is some form of pneumatic system on board.


Features

  1. CAN
    Connects the PCM to the roboRIO/PDP/PCM controller area network. Weidmuller connectors.
  2. 8 Solenoid Channels rated for 12/24V (adjusted via jumper) at a current sum of 500mA. Used to power pneumatic solenoids.
  3. Pressure switch input. Gives the PCM insight into the air pressure contained within your pneumatics system.
  4. Compressor output power channel, regulated by PCM.
All Connections are Weidmuller.

Tips

  • Changing the solenoid voltage jumper changes the voltage across ALL solenoid channels. If left floating (NO jumper), the voltage across solenoid channels will be 24V.
  • When mounting, take advantage of the holes on either corner which are perfect for a classic bolt & nut.
  • As an aside, the air compressor will most likely be the loudest component of your robotic system. It is recommended that the air compressor be mounted AWAY from your control system board, near the tanks, and done so securely. The vibrations can loosen a standard nut from a bolt, as such, systems mounted near the air compressor using a bolt & nut should utilize lock nuts and lock washers.

Voltage Regulator Module (VRM)

The VRM is an independent module that is powered by 12 volts. The device is wired to a dedicated connector on the PDP. The module has multiple regulated 12V and 5V outputs. The purpose of the VRM is to provide regulated power for the robot radio, custom circuits, and IP vision cameras.

A valuable component for powering low voltage device. True to its namesake, it drops the voltage across two sets of rails featuring channels for 12V and 5V. This module is famous in FRC robotic systems for powering the included OpenMesh radio, custom sensor circuits, an Arduino, and even LED lighting.
